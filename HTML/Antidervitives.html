Antidifferentiate Sums/Differences of Powers

Antidifferentiating sums or differences of powers in calculus can be represented using the integral sign (∫) and appropriate notation for the function. Here's an example using keyboard characters:

∫(x^n + x^m) dx = (x^(n+1))/(n+1) + (x^(m+1))/(m+1) + C

In this expression, ∫ represents the integral, dx represents the variable of integration (often x), and C represents the constant of integration. The function is given by a sum of two terms, x raised to the power of n and x raised to the power of m. To antidifferentiate this expression, each term is raised to the power of its original exponent plus one, and then divided by the new exponent plus one. Finally, the constant of integration is added.

Note that this notation assumes x is the independent variable and n, m, and C are constants.
